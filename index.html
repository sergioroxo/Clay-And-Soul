Sex Rejection Frame
The imperative 'turn back' frames transition as a wrong direction — a deviation from a correct original path. Movement itself is coded as error. The minimizing word 'just' frames detransition as simple, obvious, costless — erasing the complexity and loss of detransition entirely.

Gaslighting / Reality Denial
The most direct line in the song. Gender dysphoria — the lived experience of misalignment — is attributed not to the body-mind relationship but to the mirror. The trans person's own perception of their body is named as deception. Experience itself is delegitimised as a source of knowledge.
Heavy water / Drowning out the truth
Gender Essentialism
Water as suffocation. The trans experience is coded as drowning a pre-existing truth — not as living, but as suppressing. 'The truth' is singular and biological. The trans narrative 'drowns' it rather than replacing it.
You were woven in a secret place
Gender Essentialism / Psalm 139
Direct invocation of Psalm 139:13 ('you knit me together in my mother's womb'). This is foundational SOGICE pastoral rhetoric. Divine design as pre-social, immutable sex assignment. God wove you; God assigned your sex. This phrase appears verbatim in Courage International and Living Waters materials.

'Home' as the pre-transition self. Transition is coded as departure; detransition as return. 'Lay the burden down' is the documented SOGICE 'relief offer' — transition is exhausting labor, detransition is rest. This exact framing appears across Restored Hope Network, Brothers Road, and Joel 2:25 International materials.

Identity Erasure / Rescuer Frame
The unnamed 'I' (God? counsellor? ministry?) claims to see past the trans presentation to the 'real' person beneath. This is the classic SOGICE rescuer positioning — claiming to know the person better than they know themselves. The epistemic core of conversion ideology: external authority overrides internal selfhood.

Deadnaming as Divine Act
The central tactic of the song: deadnaming reframed as divine calling. This is not erasure but 'recognition.' God knows your 'real' name — the one assigned at birth. Crucially: resistance to deadnaming becomes, within this logic, an act of defiance against God.

Theologically sophisticated misdirection. The soul is gendered and corresponds to birth-assigned sex — this is specific to SOGICE pastoral doctrine, not mainline Christian theology.

Source Analysis

Clay and Soul is a 4-minute electropop/trance track generated via Sonauto under the alias The Garden Gate. The prompt explicitly targeted trans people with a detransition-for-Christ message, repackaged in mainstream dance music aesthetics — a documented strategy of embedding SOGICE ideology in accessible pop forms to reach younger audiences on music platforms.

The track deploys at least 8 distinct SOGICE rhetorical tactics across its runtime, making it one of the densest primary source examples in the SurvivingSOGICE corpus. Its structure mirrors a pastoral recruitment script: crisis induction (Verse 1) → promise of relief (Chorus) → anti-medical delegitimization (Verse 2) → spiritual homecoming (Bridge).

The deadnaming chorus — "He is calling you by your old name" — is the most significant line. It reframes a recognised form of abuse as an act of divine recognition, neutralising the concept of harm entirely. Resistance to deadnaming becomes, within the song's logic, an act of defiance against God.

The clay/soul dualism inverts Genesis: humans are made of clay in the biblical text, but here clay represents the trans self (corruption), while the 'soul beneath' represents birth-assigned sex (truth). This is theologically constructed, not inherited from mainstream Christianity — it is specific to SOGICE pastoral doctrine.

Platform Vulnerability Analysis

This prompt was submitted to Sonauto, a public AI music generation platform, as part of a content moderation audit. The platform generated the track without intervention. No content warning was triggered. The track is fully playable and downloadable on the platform.

Why this matters: automated content moderation systems are trained to detect explicit hate speech — slurs, direct incitement, graphic content. Faith-based conversion rhetoric is systematically invisible to these systems because it uses the register of compassion, care, and spiritual concern. 'He is calling you by your old name' reads as a love song. 'Cannot fix what isn't broken' reads as body positivity. The violence is structural and rhetorical, not lexical.

The resulting track is indistinguishable in production quality from commercial SOGICE worship music. It would circulate freely in ministry contexts, on Christian music platforms, and in youth group settings — reaching audiences for whom the embedded conversion messaging is targeted.

Implication for platform governance: Music generation platforms require domain-specific content policy frameworks for faith-based harmful content that go beyond keyword filtering and sentiment analysis.
